# Attestation: Make the Product Backlog DEEP — Roman Pichler

## Summary

Roman Pichler's blog post introduces the DEEP acronym as a quality framework for product backlogs: Detailed appropriately, Estimated, Emergent, and Prioritized. The post argues that backlogs become harmful when they accumulate excessive detail uniformly across all items, and that maintenance (grooming/refinement) must be continuous rather than a one-time activity.

## Key passages

**Detailed appropriately**: Higher-priority items should carry substantially more detail than lower-priority ones. Pichler quotes Schwaber and Beedle: "The lower the priority, the less detail, until you can barely make out the backlog item." The principle: detail level should inversely correlate with priority ranking.

**Emergent**: The backlog possesses an organic, evolutionary quality. Contents change regularly based on customer and user feedback. Items are continuously added (new ideas), modified (refined based on learning), reprioritized (adjusted as circumstances change), and **removed (when no longer relevant)**.

**Estimated**: All backlog items—particularly those aligned with the current product goal—require estimates. These support prioritization decisions, progress tracking, and capacity planning.

**Prioritized**: All items must be ordered, with highest-priority items at the top. Recommended prioritization sequence: risk first, then cost-benefit analysis, then dependencies. Once completed, items are removed from the backlog.

**Ongoing maintenance**: To maintain DEEP characteristics, the product owner and development team must regularly refine the backlog through collaborative, continuous refinement—not a one-time activity.

**Anti-pattern warning**: Large, heavily detailed upfront backlogs often function as disguised requirements specifications, reducing adaptability and likelihood of product success.

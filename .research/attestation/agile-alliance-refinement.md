# Attestation: Backlog Refinement — Agile Alliance Glossary

## Summary

The Agile Alliance glossary entry for backlog refinement covers the terminology evolution from "grooming" to "refinement," the core activities, participation, cadence, and the scope-management function of refinement. It documents the historical origins (Mike Cohn 2005, Kane Mar 2008, Scrum Guide 2011) and includes explicit mention of removing irrelevant user stories as a core refinement activity.

## Key passages

**Terminology evolution**: The term shifted from "backlog grooming" to "backlog refinement" due to "increasingly negative connotation of the word grooming." Originally, grooming imagery reflected "trimming, pruning, and cleaning, as with a plant"—an organic maintenance approach.

**Refinement activities**: During refinement sessions, teams:
- Remove user stories that no longer seem relevant
- Create new stories addressing newly discovered needs
- Re-assess relative story priority
- Assign estimates to unestimated stories
- Correct estimates based on new information
- Split high-priority stories that are too large for upcoming iterations

**Participation and cadence**: The product owner leads refinement with "some, or all, of the rest of the team." No rigid scheduling is prescribed; the emphasis is on maintaining "a sufficient number of stories ready for scheduling in the next few iterations."

**Scope management**: Refinement combats scope creep from stories that seemed "good ideas at the time" but lack substantial value. Without active management, backlogs accumulate items causing "schedule and budget overruns."

**Historical milestones**:
- 2005: First recorded use by Mike Cohn
- 2008: Kane Mar formally described "Story Time" as a regular meeting
- 2011: Scrum Guide officially recognized the practice

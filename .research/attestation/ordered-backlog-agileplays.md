# Ordered Backlog and Ruthless Prioritisation (Agile Plays)

## Summary

An argument for managing the backlog as a strictly ordered list rather than a categorically prioritized set. Each item's position in the list is its priority; no two items share a rank.

## Key passages

**Core argument:** Backlogs should be "strictly ordered lists" where each item occupies a unique position. "The tenth item on the list is above the eleventh and we would aim to do it first of the two."

**Problems with categorical systems:**
1. Equality within categories: items in the same category (e.g., "High Priority") lack relative ordering, creating ambiguity about which to tackle first.
2. False discontinuity: large gaps between categories cause "category creep," where most work becomes classified as high-priority, leading to constant reprioritization.

**Key principle:** When new work enters the backlog, its placement makes two explicit statements: it is less critical than items above (which will not be interrupted) and more critical than items below (which will be delayed). "When everything is a priority, nothing is a priority."

**Automation note:** The content emphasizes disciplined manual ordering and decision-making processes; no discussion of automated tooling or machine-readable ordering systems appears in the source.

## Structural notes

- Position-as-priority is the purest machine-readable form: a queue consumer reads items top-to-bottom without needing to parse or interpret a priority field value.
- The ordering itself is a human act; the machine-readability is a property of the output structure, not the mechanism that produced it.
- Converges with Hartman (agileforall) and Agile Fixer on the same structural recommendation from different angles.

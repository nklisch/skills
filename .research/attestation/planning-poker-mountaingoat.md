# Planning Poker: An Agile Estimating and Planning Technique (Mountain Goat Software)

## Summary

Mike Cohn's canonical description of Planning Poker — the process, card values, simultaneous reveal, and the role of discussion in improving estimate accuracy. The source focuses on estimation, not prioritization.

## Key passages

**Card values:** Decks display "1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, 20, 40, and 100" (a modified Fibonacci sequence). These represent story points or other estimation units.

**Process:** Product owner presents a user story; team asks clarifying questions; each estimator independently selects a card; all cards revealed simultaneously; high and low estimators explain their reasoning; team re-estimates until consensus.

**Simultaneous reveal:** Prevents anchoring bias — no single estimator's opinion sets the anchor before others have formed their own view.

**Discussion value:** "The high and low estimators, especially, should share their reasons." Research cited as supporting the effectiveness of this discussion for improving accuracy, particularly for uncertain items.

**Cohn's warning against averaging:** "Don't Average During Planning Poker" — averaging is presented as an error; consensus through discussion is the goal.

**Story points and prioritization:** The source does not address the relationship between story points and prioritization. Story points are presented purely as relative effort estimates.

## Structural notes

- Planning Poker is explicitly an estimation technique, not a prioritization mechanism.
- Story points estimated via Planning Poker inform velocity calculations, which can affect sprint capacity planning — but this is indirect prioritization at best.
- The social dynamics (discussion, justification, re-estimation) are intrinsic to the technique; they cannot be removed without changing what the technique does.

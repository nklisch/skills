# Agile Antipattern: Everything is Priority 1 (Agile for All)

## Summary

Bob Hartman's description of the "everything is P1" antipattern: why categorical priority flags inflate, how the inflation makes the backlog unusable for ordering, and the recommended alternative of a strictly ordered (ranked 1-N) list.

## Key passages

**The antipattern:** Organizations claiming to maintain a "prioritized product backlog" frequently have "the top 97 [items] as priority 1, the next 3 as priority 2 and no priority 3 items" — described as "a useless list."

**Why it happens:** The terminology "prioritized" has been overloaded. The real cause is that within any categorical priority system, "there's rarely anything in a priority flag system that limits the number of allowed P1s." Stakeholders rationally assign top priority because lower-priority items are unlikely to get done.

**Consequences for work ordering:**
- Teams lose the ability to learn from completed work before building subsequent items.
- The most important features may not reach release because there is no definitive top-to-bottom ordering.
- Teams cannot make informed adjustments to the backlog based on delivered results.

**Hartman's explicit outcome:** "In the worst case virtually everything ends up as a P1, making it utterly unclear what really needs to be done first, causing the whole system to collapse."

**Recommended alternative:** Abandon "prioritized product backlog" language in favor of "RANKED backlog, where the rankings go from 1-N and there are no ties." Teams consistently work on "the next item in the ranking, not just work on some random thing which seems interesting."

**Forced trade-offs:** The ranked list mechanism forces explicit trade-offs — adding a new item requires deciding where it sits relative to every existing item.

## Structural notes

- Hartman's critique applies to all categorical priority schemes (P1/P2/P3, High/Medium/Low, and by extension MoSCoW categories).
- The 1-N ranked list is the structural alternative to flag-based priority; it is machine-readable in the sense that position IS priority.
- The 1-N approach requires continuous human re-ordering as new items arrive — the ordering is the ceremony, not a pre-computed field.

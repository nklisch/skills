# Attestation: 27 Product Backlog and Refinement Anti-Patterns — Age of Product

## Summary

A numbered catalog of product backlog anti-patterns, covering both backlog content problems (over-size, outdated items, idea storage) and refinement process problems (too much refinement, premature detailing, insufficient team involvement). The article introduces the "Anti-Product Backlog" concept as a transparent repository for rejected ideas.

## Key passages

**Anti-Pattern #2 — Over-Sized**: Backlogs containing more items than deliverable within 3–6 sprints create waste. Teams refine items never converted to Increments, and sunk cost fallacy compromises value delivery.

**Anti-Pattern #3 — Outdated Issues**: Items untouched for several weeks (typically 3–4 sprints) become obsolete. Product Owner hoarding creates risk that "older items become outdated, thus rendering previously invested work of the Scrum team obsolete."

**Anti-Pattern #4 — Everything is Detailed and Estimated**: Teams practicing excessive upfront work by detailing and estimating all Product Backlog items face misallocated effort. Refinement should be "a continuous effort only to the point where the Scrum team feels comfortable turning these items into Increments."

**Anti-Pattern #5 — User Story Author**: Product Owners who invest excessive upfront effort creating overly detailed items reduce developer engagement. When work items appear complete, developers skip refinement participation, compromising shared understanding.

**Anti-Pattern #12 — Storing Ideas**: Using Product Backlog as idea repository creates noise obscuring signal. Large backlogs suggest organizational busyness rather than customer value. The article states ideas belong in product discovery systems, not the backlog.

**Anti-Pattern #23 — Too Much Refinement**: The Scrum team has too many refinement sessions, resulting in a too detailed Product Backlog. This represents analysis paralysis; additional refinement efforts yield diminishing or negative returns. Key insight: "The only way for a Scrum team to understand whether the previous validation of the underlying hypotheses of a new feature is correct is to build and ship this thing."

**Anti-Pattern #24 — No Time for Refinement**: Insufficient refinement sessions produce low-quality backlogs. The Scrum Guide recommends allocating up to 10% of team capacity to refinement—"Nothing is more expensive than an ill-designed feature delivering little or no value."

**Anti-Pattern #25 — Involving Scrum Team**: Limiting refinement participants to lead engineers/designers increases confirmation bias risk.

**Anti-Pattern #26 — Over-sized Product Backlog**: Massively comprehensive backlogs function as "inventory of work-items"—an anti-pattern. Healthy backlogs span 3–6 sprints.

**Anti-Product Backlog concept**: A "living repository of issues that a Scrum team decides not to pursue," functioning as transparent tracking for rejected ideas and hypotheses. Recommended as an alternative to simply deleting items.

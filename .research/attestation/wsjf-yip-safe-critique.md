# Problems I Have with SAFe-Style WSJF (Jason Yip, Medium)

## Summary

A critique by Jason Yip of how SAFe adapts Reinertsen's CD3 into a relative-scoring system. The article argues that SAFe's three-proxy approach (User-Business Value + Time Criticality + Risk Reduction) loses the economic grounding that makes CD3 useful for trade-off decisions.

## Key passages

**Loss of decision-making utility:** When Cost of Delay becomes a dimensionless relative number (e.g., "9"), it cannot support actual economic trade-offs. Yip cites Reinertsen directly: "We simply have no business trading money for cycle time if we do not know the economic value." A dimensionless score cannot ground this trade.

**Redundant information architecture:** Breaking down CoD into separate sub-components (Business Value, Time Criticality, Risk Reduction) is theoretically unnecessary because Cost of Delay mathematically encompasses all of these. Adding sub-components introduces noise without theoretical justification.

**Oversimplification masking complexity:** Relative scaling may produce reasonable approximations but the three-parameter structure adds complexity compared to directly estimating CoD.

**What the article does NOT address:** Gaming risks, sandbagging behaviors, or ongoing human judgment requirements — though loss-of-precision concerns gesture toward these.

## Structural notes

- Positions Reinertsen's CD3 as the theoretically grounded baseline; SAFe WSJF as a degraded proxy.
- The core problem: removing the economic unit (money/time) from the numerator breaks the mechanism that makes CD3 a genuinely machine-sortable score in Reinertsen's formulation.
- Yip's critique implies that SAFe WSJF scores are comparatively weaker candidates for automated ordering because the scores lack the absolute economic grounding that would make their ordinal ranking meaningful over time.

# Attestation: Cost of Delay — Planview

## Summary

A practitioner-oriented explanation of Cost of Delay (CoD) as a prioritization metric. Defines CoD as a measure of the impact of time on outcomes—specifically, how much the time spent waiting in a backlog costs the business. Demonstrates through examples how sequential delivery incurs lower total delay cost than simultaneous development. Connects CoD to Lean principles. Does not cover the four urgency archetypes from Don Reinertsen's framework in this article.

## Key passages

**Definition**: Cost of Delay "helps you understand and quantify the impact of time on outcomes." More specifically, it "offers a simple way to calculate how much the time it takes to develop a new feature, including any time spent waiting in a backlog, will end up costing your business." The approach considers "both value and urgency."

**Queue economics**: When items wait, organizations "delay realizing the value of each feature until it and any features that come before it are delivered." This creates compounding economic harm—each week a feature remains in the queue, the organization loses its full monetary value.

**Numerical example**: Doing everything simultaneously incurs $414,000 in costs; strategic prioritization reduces this to $254,500. The difference represents the economic value of thoughtful queue management.

**Economic harm types**: Beyond direct delay cost, the article identifies: lost "competitive advantage," damaged "customer trust," and compromised "return on investment" when features age in backlogs.

**Lean connection**: Briefly mentions that "embracing Lean principles for project management can help reduce waste and improve overall planning." Does not develop queue theory or establish explicit connections to flow economics frameworks in this article.

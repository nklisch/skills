# MoSCoW Prioritization: History, Overview, and Critical Analysis (Horkan)

## Summary

A critical analysis of the MoSCoW method covering its origin in the DSDM ecosystem and three main failure modes: subjectivity in boundary-setting, priority inflation within categories, and context blindness.

## Key passages

**History:** MoSCoW developed in the 1990s within software development, becoming closely associated with DSDM. The article describes it as emerging "in response to the increasing need for fast-paced and adaptive planning techniques."

**Critique 1 – Subjectivity:** The framework is "susceptible to biases and subjectivity in decision-making," particularly in sorting items across the four categories. No objective criteria reliably distinguish Should from Could.

**Critique 2 – Priority inflation:** "A lack of stringent criteria may lead to everything being labeled a 'Must-Have' or 'Could-Have', thereby diluting the framework's efficacy." The method lacks built-in mechanisms preventing category drift toward the top.

**Critique 3 – Context blindness:** MoSCoW "does not inherently consider external factors like market conditions or regulatory requirements," producing classifications somewhat divorced from operational realities.

**Machine-readable vs. ceremony:** The article does not use this framing explicitly, but implies that MoSCoW's effectiveness depends heavily on stakeholder alignment and subjective judgment rather than objective, automatable criteria.

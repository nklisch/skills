# Attestation: Google's Open Knowledge Format and the problems it deliberately doesn't solve

## Summary

Thor Henning Hetland's June 17, 2026 critical analysis (5 days after the
announcement). Central thesis: OKF is "good news" but "stopped exactly where it
gets hard" — it deliberately does not solve three problems the author names as
hard: trust/verification, contradiction handling, and governance. The piece is
the most substantive critical response in the survey, from a practitioner whose
broader writing (Knowledge Context Protocol, "Defendable Agents") centers exactly
those gaps.

## Key passages

**The thesis (stopped where it gets hard):**

> On June 12, 2026, Google Cloud published the Open Knowledge Format (OKF)…
> My first reaction was: this is good news. My second reaction was: they stopped
> exactly where it gets hard.

**On trust (the gap the source names):**

> OKF has no trust model… trust is implicit — if it's in the repo, it passed
> through the deployment pipeline… [but for] a trust profile presented to a
> customer's compliance agent — implicit trust is not sufficient.

**The three deliberately-unsolved problems:** "it doesn't backfill the three
hard problems for you" — named across the piece as trust and verification;
contradiction handling; governance/trust & access control.

**The convergence signal:** the author reads OKF as part of a convergence
toward "knowledge-as-markdown" and treats the unsolved problems as the space
where complementary layers (his KCP, others) will sit.

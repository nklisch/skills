# Attestation: Google's Open Knowledge Format and the problems it deliberately doesn't solve

## Summary

Thor Henning Hetland's June 17, 2026 critical analysis (5 days after the
announcement). The central thesis: OKF is "good news" but "stopped exactly where
it gets hard" — it deliberately does not solve three problems the author names
as hard: trust/verification, contradiction handling, and governance. The piece is
the most substantive critical response found in the survey, from a practitioner
whose broader writing (Knowledge Context Protocol, "Defendable Agents") centers
exactly those gaps.

## Key passages

**The thesis (stopped where it gets hard):** "On June 12, 2026, Google Cloud
published the Open Knowledge Format (OKF)… My first reaction was: this is good
news. My second reaction was: they stopped exactly where it gets hard."

**The three deliberately-unsolved problems:** "it doesn't backfill the three hard
problems for you" — named across the piece as: trust and verification;
contradiction handling; governance/trust & access control.

**On trust (the most load-bearing gap for ARD's purposes):** "OKF has no trust
model… trust is implicit — if it's in the repo, it passed through the deployment
pipeline… [but for] a trust profile presented to a customer's compliance agent —
implicit trust is not sufficient." The author positions this as a deliberate
scoping choice, not an oversight: OKF specifies the *format*, not the
*trust/governance layer* that sits on top.

**The convergence signal:** the author reads OKF as part of a convergence
toward "knowledge-as-markdown" and treats the unsolved problems as the space
where complementary layers (his KCP, others) will sit.

## Summary

This chapter (Lumen Learning / SUNY English Composition II) provides a scholarly pedagogical
treatment of Stephen Toulmin's argument model as originally published in "The Uses of Argument"
(Cambridge University Press; the source cites the 1969 edition, consistent with the original
1958 publication and its subsequent Cambridge reprints). Toulmin identified six elements of
argument: Claim, Grounds (also called Data), Warrant, Backing, Qualifier, and Rebuttal. The
source's treatment of the Warrant is load-bearing for this facet: it describes the warrant as
the logical bridge that "links data and other grounds to a claim, legitimizing the claim by
showing the grounds to be relevant." The source also confirms the model's recognition as
"Toulmin's most influential work, particularly in the fields of rhetoric, communication, and
computer science."

A supplementary fetch of the NIH/PMC article (PMC8680349) on computational argument analysis
confirmed the same six-element structure and the use of the model in NLP and knowledge
representation contexts. That source cites "Toulmin, 2003" (the updated Cambridge edition).

**Bibliographic note:** Toulmin, S.E. (1958/1969). *The Uses of Argument.* Cambridge University
Press, Cambridge, England. The 2003 updated edition is widely cited in computational work.

## Structural metadata

- **Primary source:** Toulmin, S.E. (1958). *The Uses of Argument.* Cambridge University Press.
  (Lumen Learning cites the 1969 edition; PMC cites the 2003 updated edition.)
- **Six elements:** Claim, Grounds (Data), Warrant, Backing, Qualifier, Rebuttal
- **Core triad:** Claim + Grounds + Warrant (the three elements whose omission produces an
  incomplete argument)
- **Warrant function:** logical bridge — explains *why* the data supports the claim; may be
  implicit or explicit
- **Backing function:** supports the warrant itself when the warrant cannot be assumed shared
- **Qualifier function:** modal hedge (probably, usually, most) scoping claim universality
- **Rebuttal function:** conditions under which the claim does not hold
